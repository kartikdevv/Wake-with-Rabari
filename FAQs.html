FAQs

  • The Rabari tribe practices a syncretic form of Hinduism infused with their unique customs and beliefs. They hold Lord Shiva in high regard and also worship their livestock.

  • Rabari embroidery is not only an artistic expression but also a means of livelihood for Rabari women. It involves intricate designs, often featuring mirrors, beads, and vibrant threads, and is commonly used to adorn clothing and textiles.

  • While some Rabari families maintain a semi-nomadic way of life, a significant number have settled in villages due to changing economic and social dynamics.

  • The Rabari tribe confronts challenges such as the loss of grazing lands, limited access to education and healthcare, social discrimination, and the erosion of their traditional livelihoods.

  • Yes, the Indian government recognizes the Rabari tribe as a Scheduled Tribe under the Indian Constitution. This recognition affords them certain constitutional safeguards and benefits to protect their interests.

  • Various non-governmental organizations (NGOs), cultural institutions, and government initiatives are actively working to preserve and promote Rabari culture. These efforts often encompass providing education and healthcare services to the community.
