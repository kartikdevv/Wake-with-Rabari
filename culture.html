The origins of the Rabari tribe are steeped in history, mythology, and migration. According to Rabari folklore, they trace their ancestry back to Lord Shiva, one of the principal deities in Hinduism. They believe that they migrated from Iran to India several centuries ago, settling primarily in the northwestern regions of India, including Gujarat, Rajasthan, and parts of Haryana and Punjab. Over time, they adapted to the arid desert landscapes and evolved a distinctive way of life.

The Rabari tribe is a pastoral community that is found in the western Indian state of Gujarat. They are known for their unique cultural practices, which have been passed down from generation to generation. The Rabari are known for their skill in herding and breeding livestock, especially camels, and they are recognized for their traditional attire, which is embroidered with intricate designs and patterns.

Despite their differences, the Rabari share a common language, which is a dialect of Gujarati, and they all follow a similar way of life. They are known for being a proud and self-sufficient people, who value their independence and their traditional way of life.

The Rabari are also known for their traditional attire, which is both practical and beautiful. Men typically wear a dhoti, a long piece of cloth that is wrapped around the waist and legs, and a turban, while women wear a ghagra choli, which is a long skirt and blouse, and a dupatta, which is a scarf. Both men and women wear jewelry, including necklaces, bracelets, and earrings, which are often made from silver or gold.

The Rabari are also known for their skill in herding and breeding livestock. They are particularly known for their expertise in breeding camels, which are used for transportation, milk, and meat. The Rabari are also skilled in raising sheep, goats, and cows, and they often travel long distances to find grazing land for their animals.
